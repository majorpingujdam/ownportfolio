01 Overview

Reframing a rehabilitation exoskeleton's core gap — from hardware performance to service continuity.

At Beijing Ai-Robotics Technology Co., Ltd. (北京大艾机器人), I worked across user research and service design. By combining a 103-user multi-region study, a benchmark of 20+ comparable rehabilitation devices, and the company's own site-error data, I shifted the team's definition of AiWalker's most critical unmet need — away from "which hardware feature to add next" and toward "how to sustain a patient's rehab once they leave the clinic and continue at home."

The output: a clinic-to-home continuity-of-care service blueprint and four service touchpoints designed to close that gap.

02 Company & Context

A device built for the clinic, increasingly used at home.

Beijing Ai-Robotics (北京大艾机器人) develops lower-limb rehabilitation exoskeletons. Its AiWalker line spans the full recovery arc — early in-hospital, medical and dual-foot types, home and walk-assist types, plus a pediatric model — serving patients with spinal-cord injury, stroke, and cerebral palsy. The company has supported more than 240,000 patients and supplied the exoskeleton for the 2022 Beijing Winter Olympics torch relay.

As tiered diagnosis and home-based rehabilitation become policy and market trends, these devices are migrating from a professional clinical setting into everyday home life. That migration hides a critical shift: the device's real operator changes from a trained physical therapist to a family member with no medical background. That tension is exactly what this project set out to solve.

03 My Role

User-side research and service-design output.

I worked in the Product & User Research Group as a Product Service Design Assistant, reporting to the Product Lead. My day-to-day fell into three streams — at first separate, but ultimately converging into a single line of inquiry:

  • User research. Published survey questionnaires to 103 users across different regions and collected backend feedback, quantifying real experience and drop-off at each stage of the rehabilitation journey.
  • Product experience & feedback system. Owned the company's main site UI and error-reporting system — resolving bugs, testing UX logic, and maintaining the error/feedback data.
  • Competitive & market research. Benchmarked 20+ comparable rehabilitation devices and companies to inform the product's service positioning.
04 Research

Three streams of evidence pointed to the same gap.

4.1   Multi-region user study (103 users)

The cross-region detail matters in China, where rehab access differs sharply between tier-1 hospitals and everywhere else. Split by stage, a counter-intuitive finding emerged: drop-off peaks not during therapist-supervised in-clinic use, but at the discharge handoff — when patients go home to continue on their own. The hardware was fine; many households stopped using it within weeks.

4.2   Competitive analysis (20+ devices)

After reviewing 20-plus competitors, a clear white space appeared: nearly everyone competes on hardware specs — torque, weight, motors, gait algorithms — and almost no one competes on the service wrapper: handoff training, remote support, caregiver enablement. The industry had pinned its entire competition on hardware, while the service layer that actually determines whether home rehab continues sat unclaimed.

4.3   Site experience & error data

Because I also owned the main site UI and error system, I reframed the error logs as a behavioral signal: where users get stuck online and in the workflow often mirrors where they get stuck in the real rehabilitation journey. The site is also the first touchpoint families reach when making a purchase decision or looking for help. Layered onto the user study, this live stream of friction data made the research considerably more credible.

4.4   Field moment

Mr. Du

What truly clarified the problem was an elderly patient named Mr. Du. In the hospital, with a therapist beside him to set up and operate the device, he could use it fluently. But back home, facing the same interface alone, he could no longer use it at all.

The device had not changed — what never transferred was the therapist's operating knowledge. In that moment I realized we were not solving an interface problem; we were solving a broken-service problem.

05 The Insight

Information asymmetry at the handoff.

The therapist holds the device's operating knowledge; the patient and family do not. In the hospital this asymmetry is invisible — the therapist is always present, silently filling the missing knowledge. But once home rehab begins and the therapist steps away, the knowledge gap surfaces immediately, and the device goes unused.

So the problem was redefined: AiWalker's core gap is not in the hardware, but in service continuity across the clinic-to-home handoff. That became the design entry point for the entire solution.

07 Interventions

Four touchpoints, each closing a specific information gap.

  1. 01

    Caregiver onboarding

    A structured discharge handoff session plus a quick-reference card, so the family systematically inherits the operating knowledge the therapist holds.

    Info gap closed The knowledge gap between therapist and family.

  2. 02

    Simplified home-mode UI

    A stripped-down interface for unsupervised home use — the direct fix for the "Mr. Du" problem: an interface that worked beside a therapist, made usable by one person alone.

    Info gap closed The gap between clinical complexity and caregiver ability.

  3. 03

    Remote tele-rehab check-in

    Scheduled remote follow-ups after discharge keep the therapist in the loop instead of severing the relationship at the hospital door.

    Info gap closed The loss of ongoing professional guidance after discharge.

  4. 04

    Error-feedback loop → therapist

    Repurposing the site/error system I owned, so real friction from home use flows back to the clinician instead of dying in a backend log.

    Info gap closed The gap that keeps user struggles from reaching the professional.

08 Outcome & Reflection

From "what feature next?" to "how do we keep care continuous?"

I delivered the complete first-hand research (the 103-user multi-region study, the 20+ competitor benchmark, and the site error-data analysis) and, on that basis, produced the clinic-to-home continuity-of-care blueprint and the four-touchpoint proposal. The most direct impact was reframing the internal conversation — moving the team's focus from "what hardware feature to add in the next version" to "how to keep care continuous at home," turning a problem treated as hardware into a service-design problem.

Note: the blueprint and four touchpoints are my design synthesis and proposal based on the research, and can be stated honestly as "research delivered, blueprint proposed."

This experience confirmed my direction as a healthcare service designer. I increasingly believe that many problems in healthcare that look like "the product failed" are really "the service didn't catch the person": information asymmetry between roles, amplified at a handoff, landing on the most vulnerable user. The Mr. Du moment and the through-line of my work since — removing information asymmetry between parties — are one and the same.

It also echoes my design philosophy: bridge disciplines, embrace cultures, restore dignity. Good service design ends with one outcome — an elderly man, alone at home, able to use the technology meant to help him, with dignity and without depending on anyone else.
