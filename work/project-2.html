04 Research

Three streams of evidence pointed to the same gap.

4.1 · User Study

Drop-off at discharge

In-clinic use: sustained. Post-discharge home use: collapses within weeks. The hardware was fine; the handoff wasn't.

4.2 · Competitive Analysis

Industry blind spot

20+ competitors race on hardware specs. The service wrapper — handoff training, remote support, caregiver enablement — sits almost entirely unclaimed.

4.3 · Site & Error Data

Site friction = real friction

Error logs from the site I owned mirrored where users got stuck in real rehab — a live behavioral signal layered onto the study.

4.4 · Field moment

Mr. Du

In the clinic, fluent. At home, alone — couldn't operate the device at all. The interface hadn't changed. The therapist had left. Not an interface problem: a broken-service problem.

05 The Insight

Information asymmetry at the handoff.

The therapist holds the device's operating knowledge; the patient and family do not. In the hospital this asymmetry is invisible — the therapist is always present, silently filling the gap. Once home rehab begins and the therapist steps away, the device goes unused.

AiWalker's core gap is not in the hardware — it's in service continuity across the clinic-to-home handoff.

07 Interventions

Four touchpoints, each closing a specific information gap.

  1. 01

    Caregiver onboarding

    Discharge handoff session + quick-reference card, so the family systematically inherits the therapist's operating knowledge.

    Closes →Knowledge gap between therapist and family.

  2. 02

    Simplified home-mode UI

    Stripped-down interface for unsupervised home use — the direct fix for the Mr. Du problem.

    Closes →Gap between clinical complexity and caregiver ability.

  3. 03

    Remote tele-rehab check-in

    Scheduled remote follow-ups after discharge — keeps the therapist in the loop instead of severing the relationship at the hospital door.

    Closes →Loss of ongoing professional guidance after discharge.

  4. 04

    Error-feedback loop → therapist

    Repurposed site error system so real friction from home use flows back to the clinician, not a backend log.

    Closes →Gap that keeps user struggles from reaching the professional.

08 Outcome & Reflection

From "what feature next?" to "how do we keep care continuous?"

Before

"Which hardware feature do we add in the next version?"

After

"How do we keep care continuous once the patient goes home?"

Many problems in healthcare that look like "the product failed" are really "the service didn't catch the person" — information asymmetry between roles, amplified at a handoff, landing on the most vulnerable user.

The Mr. Du moment and the through-line of my work since — removing information asymmetry between parties — are one and the same.

Note: the blueprint and four touchpoints are my design synthesis and proposal based on the research — honestly stated as "research delivered, blueprint proposed."
