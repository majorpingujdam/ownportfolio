Overview

Sky Harvest (VertiFresh) is a fruit and vegetable rooftop-cultivation and drone-delivery system that shortens the distance from farm to table. By turning unused Manhattan rooftops into micro-farms and using drones to deliver within a 1 km radius, the system cuts supply-chain steps, reduces carbon emissions, and brings personalized fresh produce to urban residents who currently lack access to it.

Urban Space Reuse Agricultural Planting Drone Delivery
Background

Urban residents face chronic delays and inefficiencies in the fresh-food supply chain. The problem is structural — not just a logistics issue, but a failure of design.

Low Transport Efficiency

Long delivery cycles reduce freshness, flavor, and nutrient content before the produce reaches consumers.

Highly Vulnerable Supply Chain

Extreme weather, transport strikes, and policy changes quickly impact availability and pricing.

Dietary Inequality

Low-income neighborhoods are dominated by fast food and ultra-processed snacks. Fresh produce is a class privilege.

No Personalization

No existing mechanism for personalized planting or product selection — users can't choose what grows for them.

Why prior models failed

Amazon Fresh Stations

~45% rent

Rent consumed ~45% of revenue. 32% freshness complaints. Persistent theft problems.

Community Bulk Orders

High minimums

High minimum orders drove user loss. Users couldn't commit to quantities they didn't need.

Commercial Rooftop Farms

~25% premium

Prices ran ~25% above market. Failed to achieve network effects at scale.

Research

Manhattan as test ground

~70,000 people per square mile creates concentrated demand. High density, diverse cultural preferences, and a fast-paced lifestyle make Manhattan ideal for piloting rooftop farms and drone delivery. The city struggles with fresh-produce access due to long-distance supply chains, limited customized options, and inconvenient traditional retail.

System Architecture

The VertiFresh system is built around a hexagonal stakeholder model — six nodes orbiting a central CORE: Farm, User, Drone, Backend, Operations, and Product. Each node has defined responsibilities and feedback loops that keep the full system in balance.

Hardware

DJI Matrice 3D

GPS-guided, obstacle avoidance, custom box mount. DJI Airport 3 dock with 20-min fast-charge and weather monitoring.

Efficiency

~40% faster charging

Rotating charging station increases turnaround efficiency. Window/balcony receiving platforms for last-meter delivery.

Safety

Full compliance

Drone law / no-fly zone compliance · DJI O3 live feed · sensor dashboard · geofencing · one-click emergency takeover · dedicated safety monitor.

Methodology · A* Shortest Delivery Route

A Python A* pathfinding model on a 10×10 grid — using heapq and math for Euclidean distance, matplotlib / numpy for visualization — computes the shortest delivery route while avoiding building obstacles. First and final route visualizations confirm optimal pathing.

App & Roadmap

VertiFresh app · Eco-Points

The app handles fresh-box selection, ordering (e.g., buy-one-get-one), and eco-point redemption. Eco-points reward both staff (planting, monitoring) and users (returning boxes, recycling) — making high-value customers feel treated specially while reinforcing a closed sustainability loop.

Summary and Reflections
Before

"The farm is somewhere far away."

After

"The farm is on your roof, and lunch arrives by air in under 20 minutes."

The key realization was that the food-supply problem isn't primarily a delivery problem — it's a distance problem. Prior models failed because they tried to optimize the wrong variable. Sky Harvest collapses the distance entirely, making freshness a structural guarantee rather than a logistical hope.
