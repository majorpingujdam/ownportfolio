01 Overview

At IKEA China (瀹滃涓浗), I joined the Visual Merchandising team that builds the showroom's vignettes 鈥?staged room-sets that let people see furniture in a lived-in context, not just on a shelf. I worked across two ends of that process: modeling kitchen layouts in 3D before they were built, and standing on the floor where customers actually met them.

Moving between the two is where the project got interesting 鈥?the staged Nordic kitchen and the kitchen a customer was picturing at home weren't always the same room.

02 Company & Context
  • IKEA doesn't really sell a chair 鈥?it sells the feeling of a room. The vignette is how that feeling gets communicated: a complete, styled scene a customer can step into and imagine living in.
  • The Visual Merchandising team owns that storytelling, translating IKEA's Scandinavian design language into scenes people connect with.
  • In China, that language meets a different way of living 鈥?different cooking, storage, and space realities 鈥?which makes the translation the most interesting part of the job.

05 The Insight

A vignette only works if the customer can picture their life in it. The further the staged room sits from how they actually live, the harder that leap becomes.

What the display intends

A complete Nordic kitchen 鈥?coordinated, aspirational, achievable. Every detail placed to tell a coherent story of a way of living.

What the customer reads

"Beautiful 鈥?but is this my kitchen? Could this work for how I cook, how my family eats, how much storage I actually need?"

Visual merchandising isn't decoration 鈥?it's communication. The gap between what a display intends and what a person actually understands is the design problem.

06 Outcome & Reflection
Before

"A room-set is a staged display."

After

"A room-set is an argument for a way of living 鈥?it succeeds or fails on whether a stranger can see themselves inside it."

The 3D models gave the team a faster way to explore layouts; the floor conversations gave those explorations a reality check. The experience reframed how I see space.

That instinct 鈥?meeting people where they actually are, not where the design assumes they should be 鈥?is the same one I carry into experience and service design.
