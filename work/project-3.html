Company Profile

IKEA China (宜家中国) is one of the world's largest home furnishing retailers, known for staging full room-sets — called vignettes — that let customers see furniture in a lived-in context rather than on a shelf. The Visual Merchandising team owns that storytelling, translating IKEA's Scandinavian design language into scenes people can imagine living in. In China, that translation meets a different way of living — different cooking habits, storage needs, and spatial realities — making the gap between design intent and customer reception the central challenge.

My Role

Visual Merchandising Intern

Reference & Research. Collected and organized 120+ pieces of material and reference data — building the visual foundation the team used to plan and justify each vignette before construction began.

3D Modeling. Built 15+ detailed Nordic-style kitchen layouts in SketchUp — giving the team a low-cost way to test configurations, proportions, and compositions before anything was physically built.

On the Floor. Provided face-to-face seasonal support for 40+ customers — using those conversations as a live signal for how people actually read, questioned, and reacted to the displays.

Design Process
01 · Model

Build in SketchUp

Test layout, proportion, and composition digitally — before committing to physical construction.

→
02 · Stage

Build the vignette

The composition becomes a physical room-set. Furniture, lighting, and props placed to tell a coherent story.

→
03 · Observe

Floor conversations

Hear how customers actually read, question, and react to the space — then carry that back into the next model.

A vignette only works if the customer can picture their life in it. The further the staged room sits from how they actually live, the harder that leap becomes.

Summary and Reflections
Before

"A room-set is a staged display."

After

"A room-set is an argument for a way of living — it works only if a stranger can see themselves inside it."

The 3D models gave the team a faster way to explore layouts; the floor conversations gave those explorations a reality check. That instinct — meeting people where they actually are, not where the design assumes they should be — is the same one I carry into experience and service design.
